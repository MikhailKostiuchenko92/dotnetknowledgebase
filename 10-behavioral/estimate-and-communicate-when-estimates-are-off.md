# Describe how you estimate and communicate when estimates are off.

**Category:** Dealing with Pressure & Tight Deadlines
**Difficulty:** 🟡 Middle
**Tags:** `estimation`, `communication`, `accountability`, `transparency`, `delivery`

## Question
> Describe how you estimate and communicate when estimates are off.

## Short Answer
I treat estimates as living commitments, not one-time predictions. When I realise an estimate is off — through new information, unexpected complexity, or discovery work — I flag it immediately with a revised number and a reason, not on the deadline day. "Here's my revised estimate and why" is always better received than "I missed it."

## What the Interviewer Is Looking For

This question assesses your **estimation maturity**, **professional communication**, and **trustworthiness**. Interviewers want to see:

- You have a structured approach to estimation, not just gut feel.
- You notice when an estimate is drifting early, not at the deadline.
- You communicate proactively — with specifics, not vague warnings.
- You take ownership of estimation errors without excessive blame or excuse.

### Estimation: Recognising Drift Early

| Signal | What It Means |
|--------|--------------|
| Discovery reveals hidden complexity | Estimate needs revision upward |
| Dependencies weren't in the original estimate | Scope is larger than planned |
| You've used more than 50% of time on less than 50% of scope | You're behind — flag now |
| Tests are taking longer than expected | Consider the impact on delivery |
| External blocker appeared | Estimate requires extension or scope cut |

> **⚠ Warning:** Flagging an estimate miss on the deadline day leaves stakeholders with no time to respond. The earlier you flag, the more options they have.

## Example STAR Answer

**Situation and Approach:**
Rather than one story, let me describe my actual workflow — it's been refined over several years and multiple misses.

**How I estimate:**

I use three-point estimation for anything over 2 days:
- Optimistic (everything goes right)
- Most likely (realistic)
- Pessimistic (one major unexpected issue)

I commit to the "most likely" estimate but plan my personal schedule for the pessimistic case. If I consistently finish at "most likely," I flag the pessimistic buffer as available capacity.

**The 50% rule:**
At the halfway mark of any estimate, I do a quick check: "Am I 50% done?" If not, I recalibrate and communicate immediately. This makes me look proactive rather than surprised.

**When estimates drift — my communication:**

I send a message structured as:
1. "My original estimate was X. It's now looking like Y."
2. "The cause is [specific reason — new complexity, dependency, discovery]."
3. "Here are my options: [A] extend by Z, [B] reduce scope to A+B, [C] other."
4. "My recommendation is [option], and I'd like your input."

**Concrete example:**
I was building an import pipeline estimated at 4 days. On day 2, I discovered the source data had 14 distinct encoding formats instead of the 2 documented. I immediately sent the PM: "Pipeline estimate was 4 days. It's now 6 days. Cause: undocumented encoding formats in source data (discovered today). Option A: 6-day delivery. Option B: support 2 formats now, remaining 12 via follow-up. My recommendation: option B — delivers 80% of value on schedule."

PM chose option B. Delivered on time for the core formats.

## Reflection / What I'd Do Differently
I would invest more in discovery spikes before committing to estimates on work involving external data or third-party systems. The hidden complexity is almost always there — I want to find it before I commit, not after.

## Common Follow-up Questions
- What estimation techniques have you found most effective?
- How do you estimate when the work is completely novel and you have no reference point?
- What do you do when you give a good-faith estimate and the team's velocity changes it?
- How do you communicate an estimate miss to a stakeholder who is not technical?
- How do you build a reputation for accurate estimation?
- What's the difference between an estimate and a commitment?

## Common Mistakes / Pitfalls
- **Single-point estimates** — "3 days" with no range or uncertainty is false precision.
- **Flagging at the deadline** — "I missed it" on deadline day is the worst possible time.
- **Vague flagging** — "I might be a bit late" without a revised number and a reason is not useful.
- **No options** — "I'll miss the deadline" gives the stakeholder nothing to work with. Always offer options.
- **Over-optimistic revision** — when you revise an estimate, add buffer; don't just extend by the known gap.
- **Estimation without post-mortem** — each significant estimate miss is a calibration opportunity. Log it and adjust.
