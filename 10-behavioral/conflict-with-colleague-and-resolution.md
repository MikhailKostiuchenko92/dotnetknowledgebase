# Describe a situation where you had a conflict with a colleague and how you resolved it.

**Category:** Conflict & Disagreement
**Difficulty:** 🟡 Middle
**Tags:** `conflict`, `interpersonal`, `communication`, `resolution`

## Question
> Describe a situation where you had a conflict with a colleague and how you resolved it.

## Short Answer
I address conflicts directly and privately rather than letting them fester. I open with curiosity — trying to understand the other person's perspective before advocating for mine — and focus the conversation on shared goals and outcomes rather than who is right or wrong. Most conflicts dissolve once both parties understand the underlying concern.

## What the Interviewer Is Looking For

This question assesses your **emotional intelligence**, **communication skills**, and **professionalism under social pressure**. Interviewers want to see:

- You don't avoid conflict or let problems accumulate.
- You approach friction as a problem to solve, not a war to win.
- You can separate the *person* from the *problem*.
- You take ownership of your part in the conflict.

### Dimensions Being Assessed

| Dimension | What a Strong Answer Shows |
|-----------|---------------------------|
| Self-awareness | You acknowledge your own role in the conflict |
| Empathy | You genuinely tried to understand the other person's viewpoint |
| Directness | You addressed the issue, not just worked around it |
| Resolution | A concrete outcome — behavioural change, repaired relationship, improved process |

> **⚠ Warning:** Never imply the conflict was entirely the other person's fault. Showing zero self-reflection is a major red flag.

## Example STAR Answer

**Situation:**
During a backend rewrite project, a senior colleague and I repeatedly clashed over code review feedback. They left very terse, sometimes dismissive comments on my PRs — e.g., "this is wrong, rewrite it" without explanation — which I found demoralising and unproductive. I noticed I was starting to dread reviews, which was affecting my output.

**Task:**
I needed to address this directly. Complaining to the manager without trying to resolve it myself felt premature, and continuing to ignore it would have damaged both the working relationship and my own productivity.

**Action:**
I asked my colleague for a one-on-one coffee chat, making it casual rather than confrontational. I opened by asking what they felt was most important in a code review — genuinely curious about their philosophy. They explained they value directness and assumed engineers would follow up if they needed context.

I then shared my perspective: I find brief feedback without reasoning hard to learn from, and asked if they would be willing to add a short "because..." after critical comments. I also offered that I would flag when I needed more context, rather than waiting.

**Result:**
They were receptive — it turned out they hadn't realised the impact. Over the following month, the quality of their feedback improved noticeably. More importantly, we built a better working relationship: I later approached them proactively for design reviews because I trusted their judgment.

## Reflection / What I'd Do Differently
I waited too long — about three weeks — before addressing it. I would act sooner now. The longer a friction point sits unaddressed, the more it colours your perception of every interaction. A two-minute conversation would have resolved it weeks earlier.

## Common Follow-up Questions
- What if the colleague had been defensive or denied there was a problem?
- How do you differentiate a personality conflict from a legitimate professional disagreement?
- What if the conflict involved someone more senior or your direct manager?
- How do you handle it when the conflict isn't about work — for example, communication style differences?
- Have you ever had a conflict that couldn't be resolved? What happened?
- What would you do if a conflict was affecting the whole team's morale?

## Common Mistakes / Pitfalls
- **Framing yourself as entirely innocent** — interviewers expect you to own some part of the dynamic.
- **"I just stayed professional"** — this sounds like avoidance. Show active resolution steps.
- **Escalating to management as the first move** — unless there's harassment or a safety issue, attempt direct resolution first.
- **Choosing a trivial story** — "we disagreed on where to have lunch" won't impress a hiring panel.
- **No long-term outcome** — describe what the relationship looked like after the conflict was resolved.
- **Venting** — describing the conflict with lingering frustration signals poor emotional processing.
