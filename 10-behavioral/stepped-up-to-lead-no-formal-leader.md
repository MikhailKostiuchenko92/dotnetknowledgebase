# Tell me about a time you stepped up to lead when no formal leader was assigned.

**Category:** Leadership & Ownership
**Difficulty:** 🟡 Middle
**Tags:** `leadership`, `initiative`, `team-coordination`, `informal-leadership`, `ownership`

## Question
> Tell me about a time you stepped up to lead when no formal leader was assigned.

## Short Answer
During a 3-week period when our tech lead was out on paternity leave and no cover was assigned, I informally coordinated the sprint, ran the daily standups, unblocked my teammates, and maintained communication with the PM. I didn't ask for permission — I identified the vacuum and filled it. Leadership is a behaviour, not a job title.

## What the Interviewer Is Looking For

This question assesses your **initiative**, **informal authority**, and ability to **create structure without mandate**. Interviewers want to see:

- You notice when coordination is missing and step up without being asked.
- You lead by service — removing blockers, facilitating decisions, communicating outward — not by dictating.
- You earn authority through competence and reliability, not title.
- You don't overstep into permanent authority; you fill a temporary gap and hand back gracefully.

### Informal vs. Formal Leadership

| Formal Leader | Informal Leader |
|---------------|----------------|
| Assigned authority | Earned authority |
| Performance management | Peer influence |
| Official communication channel | Often first point of contact by choice |
| Can make binding decisions | Makes decisions by consensus or escalation |

> **⚠ Warning:** Avoid stories where you became a de facto dictator or undermined a returning leader. Show collaborative, service-oriented leadership.

## Example STAR Answer

**Situation:**
Our team's tech lead went on extended medical leave with one day's notice. We were mid-sprint with three active PRs in review, a client demo in 8 days, and our PM was fielding questions about the sprint status that no one was answering.

**Task:**
I was the most senior remaining engineer (not the tech lead, but experienced in the codebase). No formal delegation had been made. The team was technically capable but uncoordinated — everyone was waiting for someone else to take charge.

**Action:**
On day 2 (after one day of disorganised drift), I sent a message to the team: "I'd like to help coordinate us while [tech lead] is away. I'll run standups and be the PM's first point of contact. I'll flag any decisions that need escalation to the engineering manager. Is everyone OK with that?" All agreed.

I ran daily 15-minute standups focused on one question per person: "What do you need from someone else today?" I maintained a shared blockers list in Confluence. Twice in the two weeks, I escalated architectural decisions to the engineering manager rather than making them unilaterally.

I kept the PM updated with a daily written summary: what shipped, what was in review, what was at risk. This replaced the ad-hoc requests they had been sending to individuals.

**Result:**
The client demo succeeded. All three PRs in-flight were merged within the two weeks. When our tech lead returned, I briefed them with a written summary of all decisions made, so they could step back in without disruption.

## Reflection / What I'd Do Differently
I should have asked the engineering manager for explicit delegation authority on day 1, rather than announcing it to the team directly. Even though the outcome was fine, acting without the manager's sign-off was a slight overreach in process terms. A 5-minute "here's what I'm planning to do, any concerns?" would have been the right move.

## Common Follow-up Questions
- What do you do when a more senior colleague also wants to fill the leadership gap — and their approach differs from yours?
- How do you lead without the formal authority to make final decisions?
- How do you step back into a peer role when the formal leader returns?
- Have you ever stepped up to lead and later regretted it?
- What's the difference between stepping up to lead and overstepping?
- How do you earn trust quickly when you have no formal authority?

## Common Mistakes / Pitfalls
- **Waiting to be asked** — the story is about initiative; if you only led after being designated, find a different example.
- **Over-claiming authority** — informal leadership is bounded. Show you escalated appropriately and didn't make decisions beyond your mandate.
- **No team coordination story** — "I worked hard" is not leadership. Show how you helped *others* be effective.
- **Ignoring the returning leader** — how did you hand back? This shows maturity.
- **Not communicating outward** — keeping the PM informed is part of leading a team; include it.
- **Choosing a trivial situation** — a story about leading a one-person task doesn't demonstrate leadership capacity.
