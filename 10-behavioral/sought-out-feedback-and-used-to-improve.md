# Tell me about a time you sought out feedback and used it to improve.

**Category:** Career Growth & Self-Development
**Difficulty:** 🟡 Middle
**Tags:** `feedback`, `growth`, `self-improvement`, `mentorship`, `career`

## Question
> Tell me about a time you sought out feedback and used it to improve.

## Short Answer
After a promotion cycle where I was passed over, I asked my manager directly: "What specific things would need to be true about my work for me to be promoted at the next cycle?" The feedback was specific: I needed to demonstrate ownership that spanned a full project lifecycle, not just feature delivery. I sought out a project with that scope, delivered it, and was promoted at the following cycle.

## What the Interviewer Is Looking For

This question tests **proactive career ownership and receptiveness to feedback**. Interviewers want to see:

- You seek feedback proactively, not just receive it when given.
- You ask specific questions, not "do you have any feedback for me?"
- You act on the feedback with concrete, observable changes.
- You close the loop — you followed up to confirm the change was visible.

> **⚠ Note:** "I seek feedback regularly" without a specific example is not an answer. Choose one specific instance where seeking feedback made a real difference to your development or career.

### Seeking Effective Feedback

| Principle | Description |
|-----------|-------------|
| Ask specific questions | "What specific thing would I need to do differently to be more effective in X?" beats "any feedback?" |
| Target the right person | Not the person who will tell you you're great. The person who will be honest. |
| Create the conditions | Private conversation, not a hallway moment. Give the person time to prepare. |
| Listen without defending | Your first response to feedback should be curiosity, not justification. |
| Follow through | Do something observable with the feedback — then confirm the change was noticed. |

## Example STAR Answer

**Situation:**
I was passed over for a senior engineer promotion in my second year at the company. My manager's feedback at the time was general: "you're doing good work but need more impact." I didn't fully understand what that meant.

**Task:**
Get specific, actionable feedback on what "more impact" looked like at the senior level, and close the gap.

**Action:**

*Step 1 — Ask the specific question:*
I requested a 30-minute 1:1 with my manager the following week and asked:

> "I want to make sure I'm working on the right things for the next promotion cycle. Can you tell me specifically: what would need to be true about my work over the next 6 months for you to make a strong case for my promotion?"

The feedback was specific: "You're strong on implementation. What I don't see yet is you owning a project from initial scoping through delivery and retrospective — not just the development phase."

*Step 2 — Seek calibration:*
I followed up with two senior engineers who had been recently promoted and asked them to describe one example of a project they had fully owned. This gave me a concrete picture of what "ownership" looked like at the next level.

*Step 3 — Create the opportunity:*
I identified a medium-complexity project coming up in Q3 (a self-service customer reporting feature) that had no assigned owner. I proposed to the PM that I scope and lead it. I was explicit: "I want to own this from requirements to retrospective."

*Step 4 — Execute and close the loop:*
I delivered the project — including writing the requirements document, facilitating the design review, leading the delivery, writing the post-launch post-mortem, and presenting results to the product team. I made each of these steps visible to my manager through brief updates, not just the final outcome.

At mid-year I asked my manager: "Is what I'm doing on the reporting project an example of the ownership you described? Are there gaps I should be aware of?"

**Result:**
Promoted at the following cycle. My manager's promotion write-up referenced the reporting project specifically as evidence of ownership at senior level.

## Reflection / What I'd Do Differently
I would ask the "what does promotion look like?" question earlier — not after being passed over once. Most managers will give this information if you ask. Waiting for the cycle result to trigger the conversation lost me one cycle.

## Common Follow-up Questions
- How do you handle feedback that you think is unfair or incorrect?
- How do you distinguish between actionable feedback and feedback you should set aside?
- How do you seek feedback when your manager isn't forthcoming?
- What's your approach to feedback from peers vs. managers vs. direct reports?
- How do you give yourself feedback — how do you assess your own performance honestly?
- What's the most difficult feedback you've ever received and how did you process it?

## Common Mistakes / Pitfalls
- **Asking vague questions** — "do you have any feedback?" rarely produces useful answers. Ask specific, targeted questions.
- **Defensive reception** — immediately explaining why the feedback isn't accurate signals to the giver that feedback isn't welcome.
- **No follow-through** — receiving feedback and not acting on it wastes the giver's candour and damages the relationship.
- **Not closing the loop** — acting on feedback without confirming the change is visible means you may have worked in a direction that wasn't what was needed.
- **Only seeking positive feedback** — seeking validation is natural; seeking accurate assessment is more valuable.
- **Not thanking the giver** — candid feedback is a gift. Acknowledge it explicitly.
