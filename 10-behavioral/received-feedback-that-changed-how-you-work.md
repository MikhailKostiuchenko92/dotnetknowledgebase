# Tell me about a time you received feedback that significantly changed how you work.

**Category:** Adaptability & Change
**Difficulty:** 🟢 Junior/Middle
**Tags:** `feedback`, `growth`, `self-awareness`, `improvement`, `communication`

## Question
> Tell me about a time you received feedback that significantly changed how you work.

## Short Answer
My tech lead told me that my code reviews were technically thorough but tonally harsh — juniors were deferring to me instead of defending good ideas because my comments felt like rulings rather than discussions. I changed my comment style: leading with questions instead of statements, and explicitly inviting pushback. It changed the dynamic of reviews on our team and made me a better technical communicator.

## What the Interviewer Is Looking For

This question tests **self-awareness** and **growth mindset**. Interviewers want to see:

- You can receive critical feedback without becoming defensive.
- You act on feedback with tangible, observable changes — not just "I reflected on it."
- You show that feedback made you measurably better, not just different.
- Bonus: the feedback is about a non-obvious professional blind spot, not just a skill gap.

> **Note:** This question is most powerful when the feedback is about something you didn't already know about yourself. "I was told I needed to learn X skill" is weaker than "I was told my behaviour was having an effect I didn't intend."

### Characteristics of a Strong Answer

| Characteristic | Weak Example | Strong Example |
|---------------|-------------|----------------|
| Specificity of feedback | "I needed to communicate better" | "My review comments were tonally harsh and shutting down debate" |
| Emotional honesty | "I appreciated the feedback" | "My first reaction was defensiveness — I thought I was just being precise" |
| Concrete change | "I started communicating more clearly" | "I switched to leading with questions; asking 'what did you consider X for?' instead of 'X is wrong'" |
| Measurable outcome | "My reviews improved" | "Two junior devs specifically told me they felt more confident raising disagreements in reviews" |

## Example STAR Answer

**Situation:**
During a quarterly 360 review, my tech lead shared feedback that had come from two junior developers on our team: my code review comments were technically correct but written in a way that felt dismissive. One example she shared: I had commented "This approach doesn't handle null. Use pattern matching instead." The developer had replaced the code without questioning it — even though their original approach was defensible.

**Task:**
I needed to honestly evaluate whether this feedback was accurate, and if so, make a genuine change — not a cosmetic one.

**Action:**

*Self-assessment first:*
I went back through my last 20 code review comments across two PRs. I categorised them: "instruction/correction" vs. "question/discussion." The ratio was roughly 80/20 in favour of instructions. My lead was right — I was writing reviews like a compiler, not a collaborator.

*Defining the change:*
I adopted a simple rule: if I see something I'd like changed but it isn't a clear bug, I lead with a question, not a statement. "What did you consider for the null case here? I was thinking pattern matching might be cleaner — curious if you tried it." I kept corrections as corrections for clear bugs and violations.

*Active practice:*
For the next two weeks, before posting any review comment, I re-read it and asked: "Does this invite a response or close the door?" I rewrote several comments.

*Requested follow-up feedback:*
Three weeks later, I asked my lead if she'd noticed any change. She said yes — and that one of the junior devs had pushed back on one of my suggestions in a review and been right. That hadn't happened before.

**Result:**
Review discussions on my PRs became genuine exchanges rather than acceptance queues. I received "most helpful reviewer" in an informal team survey 3 months later. More importantly, I realised that my instinct toward precision had been miscalibrated for collaborative contexts — a lesson I carry into all technical communication.

## Reflection / What I'd Do Differently
I would have asked for this kind of feedback explicitly rather than waiting for a 360 review to surface it. I now ask new teammates 30 days in: "Is there anything about how I work that surprised you or that I could do differently?" The earlier the feedback arrives, the less damage the pattern has done.

## Common Follow-up Questions
- How do you handle feedback you think is unfair or incorrect?
- How do you make sure feedback you receive leads to lasting change rather than temporary adjustment?
- Have you ever given feedback to a colleague that significantly changed how they worked?
- How do you balance being direct and precise with being collaborative and encouraging in technical discussions?
- How do you ask for feedback proactively?

## Common Mistakes / Pitfalls
- **Trivial examples** — "I was told to improve my commit messages" is a fine lesson but doesn't demonstrate self-awareness about deeper work patterns.
- **No emotional honesty** — "I immediately took the feedback on board" sounds insincere. Most people's first reaction to criticism is at least mild defensiveness — acknowledging that builds credibility.
- **Vague changes** — "I became more collaborative" doesn't tell the interviewer anything. The change must be specific and observable.
- **No outcome** — describe what was measurably different after the change, not just what you intended to do differently.
- **Feedback about a skill gap, not a blind spot** — the strongest stories are about discovering an unintended impact of your behaviour, not just learning a missing skill.
- **Blaming the feedback giver** — even a mild "although I think they took it too personally" undermines the whole answer.
