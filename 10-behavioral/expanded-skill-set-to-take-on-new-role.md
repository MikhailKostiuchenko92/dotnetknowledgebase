# Tell me about a time you had to expand your skill set to take on a new role or responsibility.

**Category:** Career Growth & Self-Development
**Difficulty:** 🔴 Senior
**Tags:** `skill-expansion`, `career`, `growth`, `leadership`, `role-transition`

## Question
> Tell me about a time you had to expand your skill set significantly to take on a new role or responsibility.

## Short Answer
When I stepped into a tech lead role for the first time, I discovered that most of what I needed was nothing I'd done as a developer: running architecture reviews, giving career feedback, writing quarterly planning documents, and facilitating alignment between engineers and product managers. I treated it as a skill acquisition challenge, not a promotion reward, and deliberately built each new capability rather than assuming it would come naturally.

## What the Interviewer Is Looking For

This question tests **adaptability at role transitions** and **deliberate skill development**. Interviewers want to see:

- You have genuine experience of a role or responsibility transition that required new capabilities.
- You identified the specific skills you needed to develop and had a strategy to develop them.
- You acknowledge that stretching into new territory is uncomfortable and that's appropriate.
- You're not one-dimensional — you can grow beyond implementation skills.

## Example STAR Answer

**Situation:**
After 3 years as a senior engineer, I was offered the tech lead role for a new 5-person team building a financial data aggregation platform. I was technically qualified; I was not prepared for most of what the role actually required.

**Task:**
Build the non-technical capabilities that tech lead work actually required: technical communication with product and leadership, team facilitation, career mentorship for direct reports, and capacity planning.

**Action:**

*First month — inventory the gap:*
I met with the outgoing tech lead and asked: "What do you spend most of your time on?" The answer was not what I expected: 40% in meetings (architecture reviews, planning, stakeholder alignment), 30% in documentation and design work, 20% in 1:1s and team facilitation, 10% in code. I had been spending approximately 90% of my time coding.

I identified the skills I needed to build:
1. Running effective meetings and architecture reviews
2. Writing clear technical documents for different audiences
3. Giving developmental feedback to engineers
4. Building a quarterly plan and prioritising with a PM

*Building each skill deliberately:*

**Meetings/facilitation**: I read *The Facilitator's Guide to Participatory Decision-Making*, attended 3 of the outgoing lead's meetings before taking over, and asked for feedback on the first 3 meetings I facilitated myself.

**Technical writing**: I used Google's Technical Writing course (free) and asked my manager to review my first 3 design documents before sharing them more broadly.

**Giving feedback**: I adopted Kim Scott's *Radical Candor* framework for 1:1s and specifically tracked one piece of growth feedback per engineer per month — not just positive feedback.

**Quarterly planning**: My manager walked me through his planning process explicitly; I shadowed him in the Q3 planning cycle before running Q4 myself.

*Acknowledged the discomfort:*
The hardest moment was the first time I had to give a junior engineer feedback that their work wasn't meeting the bar. I had never done anything like that. I prepared for 30 minutes, delivered it in 10, and followed up a week later. It went reasonably well. The second time was easier.

**Result:**
12 months into the role: the team delivered a successful platform launch. My own manager's feedback: "Your technical communication and team facilitation have developed significantly. The thing I'd watch is making sure you're not optimising for harmony over directness in feedback." I appreciated the feedback — it was exactly the right calibration.

## Reflection / What I'd Do Differently
I would ask for explicit role clarity before accepting the tech lead role. "What does success look like in the first 90 days?" and "What capabilities do you most need me to develop?" are questions I should have asked before the first day. I was too excited by the opportunity to think about the preparation.

## Common Follow-up Questions
- What's the hardest capability you've had to develop as a tech lead or senior engineer?
- How do you prioritise which new skills to develop when a role change requires many new things simultaneously?
- How do you maintain technical depth while taking on more leadership responsibilities?
- What's your approach to giving developmental feedback to engineers you're mentoring?
- Have you ever stepped back from a role or responsibility because you realised you weren't ready? What happened?
- How do you know when you're ready to take on more leadership responsibility?

## Common Mistakes / Pitfalls
- **"I already knew most of what I needed"** — role transitions that require no new skills aren't expansions.
- **Only technical skill expansion** — "I learned a new cloud platform" is growth but doesn't address leadership and communication, which are the most common gaps in senior-to-lead transitions.
- **Passive development** — "I figured it out as I went" is true but not as compelling as "here's how I specifically built each new capability."
- **No struggle** — presenting the role transition as smooth suggests you either didn't actually expand your skills or you're not being honest about the difficulty.
- **Not closing the loop** — "I developed these skills" without feedback or measurement is self-assessment, not evidence.
