# Tell me about a time you advocated for a colleague or defended someone who was being overlooked.

**Category:** Diversity, Inclusion & Psychological Safety
**Difficulty:** 🟡 Middle
**Tags:** `advocacy`, `inclusion`, `fairness`, `allyship`, `team-culture`

## Question
> Tell me about a time you advocated for a colleague or defended someone who was being overlooked.

## Short Answer
A junior engineer on our team made a significant contribution to our Q3 release — she designed and implemented the notification service that shipped on time and without bugs. At the post-release retrospective, the credit went to the tech leads. I explicitly named her contribution in the retro and in the post-release company update. Recognition that isn't visible doesn't improve careers or culture.

## What the Interviewer Is Looking For

This question tests whether you **actively advocate for fairness**, not just passively avoid unfairness. Interviewers want to see:

- You notice when credit isn't being attributed fairly.
- You take concrete action — in public, when it matters — not just private sympathy.
- You understand that advocacy is not performance; it's a consistent habit.
- You do this without making it about yourself.

> **⚠ Note:** The strongest answers involve advocacy that was awkward or required courage — not just "I agreed when someone else praised my colleague." Advocacy means saying something when others aren't saying it.

## Example STAR Answer

**Situation:**
On a 7-engineer team, a junior engineer (Maya) had taken on the notification service module for a major Q3 release. She had operated semi-independently: wrote the design document, discussed it with me, built the service, wrote integration tests, and coordinated with the product team on requirements. The service shipped on time and had zero post-release incidents.

At the post-release retrospective, the Scrum master listed the Q3 highlights: "Tech leads delivered the API layer, senior engineers finished the payment integration..." — the notification service wasn't mentioned. In the company-wide Slack post by the engineering manager, the tech leads were named; Maya wasn't.

**Task:**
Make sure Maya's contribution was visible — both to the team and to leadership — without making it a political issue or making her uncomfortable.

**Action:**

*In the retrospective (public):*
When the Scrum master asked "what went well?", I said:

> "I want to specifically call out Maya's work on the notification service. She took ownership of a module that was genuinely complex, delivered it independently with excellent test coverage and zero post-release issues. That kind of ownership from a junior engineer is remarkable and directly contributed to this being a clean release."

I kept it specific and factual — not "Maya worked really hard" but "here's what she specifically delivered and why it mattered."

*After the retrospective (with the manager):*
I had a brief private conversation with the engineering manager: "I want to flag that Maya's contribution to the notification service wasn't in the company update. Her work was critical to the release. I think it's worth acknowledging her explicitly in the update or at the all-hands." The manager updated the post.

*With Maya (private):*
I told Maya what I'd done and why. I didn't make a big deal of it — "I made sure your work was credited accurately" — but I wanted her to know it had happened and that it was intentional, not accidental.

**Result:**
Maya received direct messages from two people in the organisation after the updated company post. She told me later: "I didn't realise it hadn't been mentioned. Thank you for noticing." At her next performance review, her manager cited the notification service as evidence of her readiness to grow into a mid-level role.

## Reflection / What I'd Do Differently
I would raise the pattern with the engineering manager more directly — "I've noticed that contributions from junior engineers aren't consistently credited in public forums" — as a systemic observation, not just addressing the individual incident. Systemic advocacy is more effective than individual incident-by-incident correction.

## Common Mistakes / Pitfalls
- **Private advocacy only** — telling a colleague "you're doing great work" without ensuring that credit reaches decision-makers changes nothing about their career.
- **Making it about yourself** — "Maya and I worked on the notification service..." shifts the credit rather than giving it.
- **Waiting for the right moment** — the right moment is when the omission occurs. Retroactive advocacy is weaker.
- **Not informing the person** — advocating for someone without telling them can feel paternalistic. Brief, direct communication ("I made sure your work was credited") is respectful.
- **Addressing the incident but not the system** — if under-attribution is systematic (always junior engineers, always women, always contractors), the individual fix doesn't address the root cause.
- **Over-correcting** — excessive, repeated public praise for one person can feel patronising or politically motivated. Advocacy should be proportionate and factual.
