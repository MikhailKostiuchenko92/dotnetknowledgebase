# Tell me about a time you helped a struggling team member turn things around.

**Category:** Mentorship & Growing Others
**Difficulty:** 🔴 Senior
**Tags:** `mentorship`, `leadership`, `performance`, `empathy`, `coaching`

## Question
> Tell me about a time you helped a struggling team member turn things around.

## Short Answer
A senior developer on my team had become disengaged — velocity dropped, reviews were perfunctory, they'd stopped contributing to architecture discussions. Rather than escalating to their manager, I had an honest one-on-one. The root cause was invisible: they felt their career had stalled and didn't see a path forward. I worked with them to identify and lead a project that reignited their motivation. Three months later, they were the team's most active technical contributor.

## What the Interviewer Is Looking For

This is a **senior-level question** about recognising underperformance early, diagnosing the root cause, and coaching people back to high performance. Interviewers want to see:

- You notice performance issues without waiting to be told.
- You diagnose before prescribing — you understand *why* someone is struggling.
- You take a coaching approach, not a punitive one.
- You can distinguish between a *can't do* problem (capability) and a *won't do* problem (motivation, context).
- You know when to involve management vs. handle it directly.

### Struggling: Root Cause Categories

| Category | Signal | Approach |
|----------|--------|----------|
| Skill gap | Correct intent, wrong output | Mentoring, pairing, learning resources |
| Motivation/engagement | Correct skill, declining output | Understand what changed; reconnect to purpose |
| Personal issues | Sudden change, unlike prior behaviour | Empathy, flexibility, HR if needed |
| Misalignment | High performer in wrong role | Role discussion with manager |
| Team conflict | Friction with specific colleague | Mediation |

> **⚠ Warning:** This question is about turning someone *around* — implying a sustained challenge and a genuine improvement. A story about helping someone through one bad week is too thin.

## Example STAR Answer

**Situation:**
A senior engineer who had been an excellent performer for two years gradually became disengaged. Their PR velocity dropped by ~60% over two months, their code review comments became one-liners, and they had stopped participating in architecture discussions entirely. Other team members started noticing.

**Task:**
I was the tech lead. This person was not my direct report, but their disengagement was affecting the team's output and morale. I needed to understand what was happening before any escalation to their manager.

**Action:**

*Step 1 — One-on-one, no agenda:*
I invited them for a coffee chat — explicitly informal, "just checking in." I opened with genuine curiosity, not performance language: "I've noticed you seem less engaged lately — how are you doing?"

They opened up: they felt they'd hit a ceiling. They had been in the same role for two years, doing the same type of work, with no visible path to a more senior position. They felt overlooked and demotivated.

*Step 2 — Remove the cause, not the symptom:*
This was a motivation/alignment problem, not a capability problem. The solution wasn't a performance improvement plan — it was restoring purpose.

With their agreement, I spoke to their manager about giving them ownership of a strategic initiative they had previously suggested but had been deprioritised: a service mesh exploration for our microservices architecture. I framed it to the manager as "a high-potential engineer who needs a challenge."

*Step 3 — Visible sponsorship:*
I asked them to present their findings at our next engineering all-hands. This gave them visibility beyond the immediate team — something they had been missing.

*Step 4 — Follow-up:*
Monthly check-ins to ensure the project was progressing and they felt supported.

**Result:**
Within 6 weeks, their velocity recovered. Within 3 months, they were the most active participant in architecture discussions and had recruited two other engineers to contribute to the service mesh research. They were promoted to Principal Engineer 8 months later.

## Reflection / What I'd Do Differently
I waited too long to have the first conversation — about 6 weeks into the decline. I would check in earlier now, before the pattern is established. Disengagement is much easier to reverse early.

## Common Follow-up Questions
- How do you decide when a struggling team member needs coaching vs. performance management?
- What do you do when you've tried coaching and the person isn't improving?
- How do you handle it when a team member is struggling but doesn't want your help?
- Have you ever had to support a manager in addressing a struggling team member?
- What's the difference between low performance and disengagement?
- How do you protect your own time and the team's delivery while supporting someone who is struggling?

## Common Mistakes / Pitfalls
- **Only describing the performance problem, not the root cause** — the story must show you diagnosed *why* before acting.
- **Prescribing without listening** — assigning extra work or training before understanding the cause is often counterproductive.
- **Missing the boundary between coaching and managing** — tech leads and senior engineers support; line managers manage performance formally.
- **No measurable outcome** — "they seemed happier" is weak. Show velocity, promotion, recognition, or sustained improvement.
- **Too quick resolution** — a real turnaround takes months, not days.
- **Going to management first** — unless there's a serious performance or conduct issue, attempt direct support first.
