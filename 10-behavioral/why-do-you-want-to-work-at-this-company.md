# Why do you want to work at this company?

**Category:** Motivation & Values
**Difficulty:** 🟢 Junior/Middle
**Tags:** `motivation`, `company-fit`, `values`, `career-goals`, `interest`

## Question
> Why do you want to work at this company?

## Short Answer
This is a question with no single correct answer — it depends entirely on which company you're applying to. The answer must be specific, genuine, and connect your professional values and goals to something real and particular about the company. Generic answers ("great culture, interesting product") are immediately obvious and ineffective.

## What the Interviewer Is Looking For

This question tests **genuine motivation and cultural fit**. Interviewers want to hear:

- You've researched the company specifically — not just their public website.
- You can articulate a connection between what you care about and what this company does.
- Your motivation is professional and sustainable — not just "higher salary" (even if that's true).
- You've thought about what you want from the next stage of your career and why this company serves that.

> **⚠ Warning:** The three worst answers: (1) "I've heard great things about your culture" — generic. (2) "The compensation is competitive" — true but not an interview answer. (3) "Your product is great" — flattery without specificity.

### What Makes a Strong Answer

| Dimension | Weak | Strong |
|-----------|------|--------|
| Specificity | "Great company with interesting work" | "Your engineering blog's post on distributed rate limiting specifically addressed a problem I've been thinking about" |
| Authenticity | "I love the mission" | "I've been using your product for 2 years; I've seen the problem you're solving firsthand" |
| Career alignment | "It's a great opportunity" | "The scale you're operating at is the context where I most want to develop distributed systems skills" |
| Culture | "Strong engineering culture" | "The fact that you open-source your internal tooling tells me something real about how you think about engineering" |

## Example Framework for Crafting Your Answer

**Before the interview, research:**
1. **Engineering blog / tech talks** — what technical problems are they solving? What do their engineers write about?
2. **Product** — use it, or read about it. Understand what problems it solves and why users care.
3. **Company news** — recent fundraising, product launches, engineering hires.
4. **Job description** — what do they actually say about the team and the role?
5. **LinkedIn** — who works there? What did their engineers do before?

**Structure your answer around three specific connections:**
1. **The technical domain**: Why does this company's technical problems interest you?
2. **The mission/product**: Why does what they're building matter to you?
3. **The career alignment**: Why does this role, at this scale, fit where you want to grow?

**Example template (fill in with specific research):**

> "I've been following [Company's] engineering blog for about 18 months — specifically [specific article] gave me a new perspective on [topic]. The [specific technical problem — e.g., high-throughput data pipeline / real-time bidding / healthcare data privacy] is exactly the class of problem I want to develop expertise in. I've been building in [current domain] and I can see that working at your scale would accelerate my understanding of [specific area]. And [specific thing about the company's mission/product] — I've seen [specific evidence of why the mission matters to you]."

## Common Follow-up Questions
- What do you know about our product? Have you used it?
- What's a technical problem you've read about in our engineering blog that you found interesting?
- What are you looking for in your next role that you don't have now?
- Where do you see yourself in 3–5 years and how does this role fit?
- What would make you choose another company over ours?
- What's the one thing you most want to learn in the next 2 years?

## Common Mistakes / Pitfalls
- **Generic company praise** — "great culture and interesting work" could describe any company. If your answer could apply to any company, it's not an answer.
- **Enthusiasm without evidence** — "I'm really excited about your mission" without demonstrating you understand the mission sounds hollow.
- **Only talking about the company** — the answer must also show why *you specifically* are excited — connect it to your goals and values.
- **No research** — if you haven't read the company blog, used the product, or looked at the team's background, you will be found out in 30 seconds.
- **Telling them what you want, not why you want them** — "I want to grow as a senior engineer" doesn't say anything about why this company is the right place for that growth.
