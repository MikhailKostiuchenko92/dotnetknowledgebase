# Describe a time your team went through a significant organizational or process change. How did you adapt?

**Category:** Adaptability & Change
**Difficulty:** 🟡 Middle
**Tags:** `adaptability`, `organisational-change`, `agile`, `process`, `resilience`

## Question
> Describe a time your team went through a significant organizational or process change. How did you adapt?

## Short Answer
Our team was reorganised from a component-based squad (all backend together, all frontend together) to cross-functional product squads. I initially found it harder — I was now the only backend engineer on my squad. My adaptation: I built stronger relationships with the frontend developer and PM, and I invested time in documenting the backend domain knowledge I used to share with a room full of backend peers.

## What the Interviewer Is Looking For

This question tests **resilience**, **flexibility**, and your ability to **find value in change rather than just tolerating it**. Interviewers want to see:

- You went through a real change with real adjustment costs.
- You didn't just complain or resist — you identified what you needed to do to succeed in the new structure.
- You contributed to the team's adaptation, not just your own.
- You can reflect honestly on both the costs and benefits of the change.

### Adapting to Organisational Change: Common Challenges

| Change Type | Typical Challenge |
|-------------|------------------|
| Reorganisation | Loss of familiar relationships; learning new collaboration patterns |
| Process change (Waterfall → Agile) | Different rhythm, different communication cadence |
| New management | Different expectations; uncertainty about standing |
| Merger or acquisition | Cultural mismatch, tool consolidation |
| Remote/hybrid transition | Loss of informal communication; explicit knowledge sharing |

> **⚠ Note:** "I adapted fine" is not enough. Show *how* you adapted — the specific things you changed about your working style or practices.

## Example STAR Answer

**Situation:**
Our engineering department reorganised from functional teams (all backend engineers together, all frontend engineers together) to cross-functional product squads. My new squad had: me (backend), one frontend developer, one designer, and one PM. I was the only backend engineer on the squad, whereas before I had been surrounded by 4 other backend peers.

**Task:**
I needed to adapt both professionally and technically. Professionally: I now had to build different kinds of working relationships. Technically: I could no longer informally check in with backend colleagues about architecture decisions.

**Action:**

*Immediate steps:*
Week 1: I scheduled 30-minute "getting to know your work" meetings with each member of the new squad — not social catch-ups, but genuine questions about what they were working on, what they found hard, and what they needed from me. I was surprised to learn the frontend developer was frustrated by unpredictable API changes — information I would never have known without asking.

*Adapting my practices:*
I couldn't rely on informal backend peer review anymore, so I introduced a practice of documenting every non-trivial backend design decision as a brief ADR in Confluence. This served two purposes: it gave me a review artifact I could share with backend engineers in other squads asynchronously, and it gave my frontend colleague context for upcoming API changes.

I also joined a cross-squad backend architecture forum (weekly, 30 minutes) to maintain technical peer relationships across the reorganised structure.

*Contributing to the squad's transition:*
I noticed the squad had no shared Definition of Done. I drafted one and presented it at our first sprint planning — it was adopted with minor edits.

**Result:**
After 3 months in the new structure, I found I actually preferred the cross-functional squad model. I had more context on product decisions, my work felt more connected to user outcomes, and I built stronger relationships with non-engineering colleagues. The ADR practice I introduced was adopted by two other squads.

## Reflection / What I'd Do Differently
I would have requested a "backend chapter" forum from day 1 rather than waiting two months to realise I was missing technical peer review. Functional communities of practice (guilds, chapters) are essential complements to cross-functional squads.

## Common Follow-up Questions
- What do you do when you disagree with a process change that's been imposed on your team?
- How do you help more resistant team members adapt to a significant change?
- What's the biggest organisational change you've experienced, and what did it teach you?
- How do you maintain technical communities of practice when teams are organised cross-functionally?
- What would you do if you were asked to lead a team through a major process change?
- How has your ability to adapt improved over the course of your career?

## Common Mistakes / Pitfalls
- **Describing the change without describing your adaptation** — the question is about *how you adapted*, not what changed.
- **Only describing negative impact** — show what you gained from the change, not just what you lost.
- **Passive adaptation** — "I just went along with it" is not adaptation; show active choices you made.
- **No contribution to team adaptation** — at the middle level and above, you should show you helped the team, not just yourself.
- **Too recent or too trivial** — the change should have required real adjustment on your part.
- **Missing the reflection** — what did you learn about yourself or your preferences through the change?
