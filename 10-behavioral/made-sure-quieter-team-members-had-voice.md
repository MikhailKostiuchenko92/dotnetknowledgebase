# Tell me about a time you made sure quieter team members had a voice in a discussion.

**Category:** Diversity, Inclusion & Psychological Safety
**Difficulty:** 🟡 Middle
**Tags:** `inclusion`, `psychological-safety`, `facilitation`, `team-dynamics`, `communication`

## Question
> Tell me about a time you made sure quieter team members had a voice in a discussion.

## Short Answer
In architecture reviews, one senior engineer consistently dominated discussion and two junior engineers never spoke. I changed the meeting format to include a round-robin input round where every person shared their view before open discussion. Within 3 sessions, the junior engineers were contributing unprompted, and one of their suggestions became a key part of our adopted design.

## What the Interviewer Is Looking For

This question tests your awareness that **great teams require deliberate facilitation**, not just good technical talent. Interviewers want to see:

- You notice when voices are missing from discussions — and you care.
- You use structural changes to the discussion format, not just encouragement ("feel free to speak up!").
- You understand that silence doesn't mean agreement or lack of ideas.
- You create conditions for contribution, not just open permission.

> **⚠ Key insight:** "Speak up if you have something to add" is not inclusion. It puts the burden on the person who is already less comfortable speaking. Structural interventions (round-robin, written input first, explicit prompts) remove the barrier without putting the individual on the spot.

## Example STAR Answer

**Situation:**
Our weekly architecture review had 8 engineers. In practice, 2 senior engineers (including myself on some topics) drove all discussion. Two junior engineers — both technically strong, based on their code — never spoke in the meeting. I noticed in 1:1s that they had opinions they were holding back ("I didn't want to seem naive").

**Task:**
Change the meeting dynamic so every engineer's perspective was heard, without putting quieter engineers on the spot.

**Action:**

*Step 1 — Understand why people weren't speaking:*
I had informal conversations with the two junior engineers. Both cited the same reason: they were intimidated by the pace of the discussion and worried their ideas would be dismissed. They also mentioned that by the time they formulated their thought, the conversation had moved on.

*Step 2 — Structural change: async written input before the meeting:*
I added a pre-meeting input phase: all engineers were invited (not required) to comment on the design document in Confluence before the meeting. The facilitator (me, rotating) would reference all written comments during the meeting — "here's what the team shared in the doc before we met."

This gave engineers time to formulate thoughts without real-time pressure.

*Step 3 — Round-robin in the meeting:*
For key decision points, I introduced a simple round-robin: "Before we decide, let's go around and everyone share one concern or one thing they like about option A." Brief, time-boxed (1 minute per person).

I modelled vulnerability in the round-robin by sharing my own uncertainty first.

*Step 4 — Explicit direct prompts (sparingly):*
For specific topics where I knew a quiet engineer had relevant experience, I'd say: "Alex, you worked on something similar at your previous company — what was your experience?" Not "do you have anything to add?" but "what was your experience?" — a question with a genuine answer.

**Result:**
After 3 sessions, both junior engineers were contributing unprompted before we reached the round-robin. One contributed a suggestion about cache invalidation strategy that the senior engineers hadn't considered and that we incorporated into the design. She later told me it was the first time she'd felt heard in a technical meeting.

## Reflection / What I'd Do Differently
I would have introduced the async written input phase at the start of the team, not 4 months in. The written input format helps everyone — not just junior engineers — by creating a record of pre-meeting thinking that prevents the loudest voice in the room from setting the direction before everyone has engaged.

## Common Mistakes / Pitfalls
- **"Speak up if you have ideas"** — this puts the burden on the individual, not the system. Structural changes are more effective than encouragement.
- **Calling on people unexpectedly** — "what do you think?" without warning can embarrass rather than include. Prepare people with pre-meeting input or explicit framing.
- **Treating silence as consensus** — silence often means "I don't feel safe to disagree," not "I agree."
- **Not noticing the pattern** — many senior engineers never recognise that the same 2 people dominate every meeting. Paying attention is the prerequisite.
- **Fixing the symptom, not the cause** — if a senior engineer is consistently dismissing contributions from junior engineers, the structural fix is incomplete without addressing that behaviour.
- **One-time effort** — inclusion in discussions requires consistent facilitation, not a one-off intervention.
