# Describe how you prioritize when you have multiple tasks with competing deadlines.

**Category:** Dealing with Pressure & Tight Deadlines
**Difficulty:** 🟢 Junior
**Tags:** `prioritisation`, `time-management`, `organisation`, `delivery`, `communication`

## Question
> Describe how you prioritize when you have multiple tasks with competing deadlines.

## Short Answer
I triage by impact and urgency: I ask "what's the business consequence if this slips 24 hours?" and rank by that answer. I keep a visible prioritised list, communicate proactively if something high-priority is at risk, and actively say no (or "not yet") to low-priority additions. The worst failure mode is quietly juggling everything and delivering nothing well.

## What the Interviewer Is Looking For

This is a 🟢 Junior question asking about your **personal organisation** and **professional communication**. Interviewers want to see:

- You have a method for prioritisation — not just "I do everything."
- You communicate proactively when priorities compete.
- You ask for help clarifying priority when it's unclear (rather than guessing).
- You don't silently overload yourself.

## Example STAR Answer

**Situation:**
During my second job, I was simultaneously responsible for: (1) a feature in active development with a sprint deadline in 3 days, (2) an urgent bug fix from QA on a different service that was blocking their testing pipeline, and (3) a code review that a teammate was waiting on to merge their PR.

**Task:**
All three had real urgency signals. I needed to sequence them without dropping any.

**Action:**

*Step 1 — Triage with a clear question:*
For each task, I asked: "What's the cost of this slipping by 1 day?" 

- Bug fix: QA pipeline blocked → whole team's velocity affected → highest urgency.
- Code review: teammate can't merge, but they can work on something else → medium urgency.
- Feature: my own sprint deadline, 3 days remaining → can absorb 2 hours now.

*Step 2 — Sequence:*
1. Bug fix first (90 minutes estimated)
2. Code review immediately after (30 minutes)
3. Feature work for the rest of the day

*Step 3 — Communicate:*
I sent a quick message to my tech lead: "Sequencing: QA blocker → PR review → feature. ETA for feature: end of day tomorrow given this diversion. Let me know if you want me to re-sequence."

They confirmed, and I had explicit alignment rather than silent assumption.

**Result:**
All three delivered on time. QA pipeline unblocked by 11am. PR reviewed by noon. Feature delivered the following afternoon, 1 day ahead of sprint deadline.

## Reflection / What I'd Do Differently
I would establish a regular 5-minute "priority check" at the start of each day — just writing down the 3 highest-impact tasks and checking if they match what I'm working on. I sometimes get pulled into an urgent-but-not-important task without this check.

## Common Follow-up Questions
- What do you do when two tasks genuinely have the same priority and urgency?
- How do you handle it when your manager and another stakeholder give you conflicting priorities?
- What tools or systems do you use to track your work and priorities?
- How do you protect focus time for deep work when you have many small interruptions?
- What do you do when someone adds a new "urgent" task to your plate mid-sprint?
- Have you ever got prioritisation wrong and paid for it? What did you learn?

## Common Mistakes / Pitfalls
- **"I just work through everything"** — this implies no prioritisation, just time-based queue.
- **Not communicating when priorities shift** — silent reprioritisation leaves stakeholders with incorrect expectations.
- **Never saying no or "not now"** — accepting every urgent request without pushback is a productivity trap.
- **No explicit method** — "I figure it out" is not reassuring. Show a simple, repeatable approach.
- **Forgetting the communication loop** — good prioritisation includes letting affected parties know what you're doing and why.
- **Confusing urgent with important** — the Eisenhower matrix exists for good reason.
