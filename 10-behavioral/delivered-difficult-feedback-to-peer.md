# Describe a time you had to deliver difficult feedback to a peer or direct report.

**Category:** Mentorship & Growing Others
**Difficulty:** 🔴 Senior
**Tags:** `feedback`, `difficult-conversations`, `leadership`, `empathy`, `accountability`

## Question
> Describe a time you had to deliver difficult feedback to a peer or direct report.

## Short Answer
I had to tell a well-liked senior engineer that their communication style — specifically, dismissive responses to junior engineers in review threads — was harming team morale and causing people to avoid asking questions. I prepared the specific examples, delivered them privately and calmly, and watched closely for whether the behaviour changed. It did. The hardest feedback I've given has usually had the most lasting impact.

## What the Interviewer Is Looking For

This is a **high-signal leadership question**. Interviewers at the senior and lead level want to see:

- You don't avoid difficult feedback out of discomfort.
- You prepare carefully and deliver it with both honesty and care.
- You focus on behaviour and impact, not personality.
- You follow up and verify that the feedback was received and led to change.
- You can maintain the working relationship through the discomfort.

### Why Difficult Feedback Gets Avoided

| Reason | Reality |
|--------|---------|
| Fear of hurting feelings | Avoiding feedback leaves the problem to grow |
| Not sure how they'll react | Preparation reduces unpredictability |
| "It'll sort itself out" | It rarely does |
| They're more senior/popular | Giving feedback upward or to respected peers is hard — and necessary |

> **⚠ Warning:** "I once told someone their code could be better" is not a difficult feedback story. The question asks for something genuinely uncomfortable — a conversation you considered not having.

## Example STAR Answer

**Situation:**
A respected senior engineer on my team — technically excellent, widely liked — had a habit of leaving very terse code review comments on junior engineers' PRs. Comments like "this is wrong, redo it" or "why would you do this?" without explanation. Over a month, I noticed junior engineers had stopped asking questions in public channels and were coming to me privately instead. One told me they were "scared" to submit PRs for this person's review.

**Task:**
I wasn't this person's manager — I was their peer. I had no formal authority, but I was the team's tech lead. The behaviour was damaging team psychological safety. I couldn't ignore it and it wasn't the manager's radar yet.

**Action:**

*Preparation:*
I documented three specific PR threads with the verbatim comments and the context. I chose examples where the junior's work was actually reasonable, making the dismissiveness clearer. I also prepared for the likely reaction: they might be surprised, defensive, or unaware.

*The conversation:*
I asked for a private coffee chat framed as "team dynamics, not a big deal, just want to check in." I opened warmly — acknowledging their technical value to the team — and then:

"I want to share something I've been noticing. In the past month, I've seen a few code review threads where the feedback was technically correct but the tone felt pretty harsh to the recipient. Specifically — [example 1, example 2]. I've heard from two junior engineers that they're hesitant to request your review because they're not sure how to interpret those comments."

I paused and let them respond. They were surprised — they hadn't realised the impact. They explained they had grown up in a "direct feedback culture" and assumed short comments were efficient.

I acknowledged their intent: "I know you're not trying to be harsh — the code feedback is usually right. What I'd ask is whether you'd be willing to add a short 'because...' to your critical comments, so the reason is visible."

*Follow-up:*
Over the next 3 weeks, I observed their reviews. Improvement was clear. I acknowledged it in a private message.

**Result:**
The junior engineers' engagement in code review gradually recovered. Three months later, the engineer whose style had changed received positive feedback in a team retrospective about "the quality of their explanations in reviews."

## Reflection / What I'd Do Differently
I would address this sooner — after the second or third incident, not after it had quietly damaged team morale for a month. Difficult feedback is like technical debt: it's cheaper to address early.

## Common Follow-up Questions
- What do you do when the person you gave feedback to becomes defensive or denies the behaviour?
- How do you deliver difficult feedback to someone who is more senior or more experienced than you?
- Have you ever given feedback that turned out to be wrong or unfair? How did you handle that?
- What's the difference between difficult feedback and constructive feedback?
- How do you decide when a behaviour issue needs to involve a manager vs. being handled peer-to-peer?
- What's the most difficult feedback you've ever received, and how did you respond?

## Common Mistakes / Pitfalls
- **Choosing a minor story** — "I told someone their variable naming was inconsistent" is not difficult feedback.
- **No specific examples** — "I told them they were being harsh" without specifics is unpersuasive to the person and unimpressive to the interviewer.
- **Waiting too long** — if you waited months to address a problem, acknowledge it as a lesson.
- **Making it about feelings vs. impact** — "it made people feel bad" is weaker than "people stopped asking questions publicly, which reduced learning for everyone."
- **No relationship repair story** — what happened to the working relationship after the feedback?
- **Skipping the follow-up** — feedback without follow-up is incomplete.
